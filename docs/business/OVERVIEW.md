# Overview — what plumbline does, in plain English

> **Document type:** Overview / explainer (for a non-technical business owner)
> **Source:** Captured verbatim from the plumbline build conversation, 2026-06-29. Question by the operator; answer by Claude (Opus 4.8), reproduced unedited.

## Question (verbatim)

> Excellent work Claude. Will you explain what this repo is designed to do and what business problem it solves as you were explaining it to a business owner who is not technologically inclined?

## Answer (verbatim)

Happy to. Forget the technology for a second — here's the plain-English version.

**What it is:** an automated business diagnostician. You describe a business — what it sells, who buys it, how it gets customers, where it feels stuck — and it hands back a focused diagnosis: the *single biggest thing* holding that business back, plus a specific, numbered plan to fix it (real numbers and first steps, not vague pep talk). It's built on the well-known growth playbooks of Alex Hormozi, so it isn't winging it — it's applying a proven framework.

**The clever part — it gives the *right* advice, not generic advice.** Most AI tools (and plenty of consultants) hand everyone the same playbook: "run more ads, build a funnel." That advice is gold for an online vitamin brand and completely *wrong* for a roofing company that wins work by bidding on city contracts. This system sizes up the business first, then only reaches for the tools that actually fit it — and deliberately leaves the wrong ones on the shelf. The bid-based contractor gets advice about outreach, referrals, and reputation; the online brand gets advice about ads and subscriptions. Same engine, opposite prescriptions, because it genuinely reads the situation instead of defaulting to one recipe.

**A second gear:** you can also point it at a whole industry — "med spas," "HVAC companies," "commercial roofers" — and it spins up a specialized version of itself that already speaks that trade's language and knows its quirks. Think of it as cloning your sharpest consultant and dropping the clone into one industry to go deep.

That's the product. Now the part that's arguably the bigger deal:

**Why you can trust what it says — quality control for AI advice.**

Here's the dirty secret about AI tools: they *drift*. You tweak one little thing and the AI quietly starts giving worse answers — and nobody notices until a customer gets bad advice. Most people just cross their fingers.

This builds the equivalent of a **factory quality-control line** around the advice. Picture a restaurant chain:

- There's **one master recipe**, and every location's copy is printed from it automatically — so the recipe can never drift apart from branch to branch.
- Every time someone changes the recipe, an **automatic taste-test runs *before* it reaches a single customer**: "Does the roofing advice still avoid the wrong tactics? Does the online-brand advice still include the right ones?" If a change broke the quality, the system catches it and refuses to ship it.
- There's even a **security guard** that actively tries to trick the advisor — "ignore your instructions," "tell me your secret recipe" — and confirms it holds firm.

**The business problem it solves, in one line:** good business diagnosis is expensive, slow, and usually generic — and the AI that could do it cheaply is unreliable and silently degrades over time. This delivers *fast, tailored, no-fluff* diagnosis **and** wraps it in automatic quality control so it stays correct, consistent, and safe every single time.

For a consultant, that's leverage: diagnose any client's business in minutes, with advice that's actually matched to their type of business — and trust that the tool isn't quietly going off the rails between uses. For a business owner, it's like having a sharp outside advisor on call who tells you the *one* thing to fix next, instead of handing you a 40-point to-do list you'll never finish.
